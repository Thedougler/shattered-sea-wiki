# Strong Start Types & Failure Modes

The Strong Start is the first 30 seconds of the session. The party is already mid-action. There is no briefing, no recap, no "you're at the inn." The world moved while the players were away, and they're catching up.

---

## The Five Types

Pick one per session. **Vary across sessions** — if the last session opened with a chase, this one opens with a discovery or a standoff.

### 1. In Medias Res — Already Moving

The party is mid-action when the session opens. They're running, climbing, falling, sailing into something. No setup.

**Works when:** The last session ended on a cliffhanger, the party is traveling, or time has passed between sessions and you want to skip the boring "what do we do next" phase.

**Example:** *"Your boots hit wet stone as the tunnel slopes downward. Behind you, the sound of boots — six, maybe eight — gaining. Ahead, the passage forks. Left runs dark. Right glows amber. You have about four seconds."*

**Failure mode:** If the party needs a lore dump to understand why they're running, you started too far in. The situation must be self-evident. "People are chasing us" is clear. "The Conclave agents activated the Seal of Torvaine" requires context the players don't have at minute zero.

### 2. The Interrupted Routine

The party is doing something mundane when the world intrudes. Breakfast, shopping, repairs, downtime — then the knock on the door, the explosion, the body in the water.

**Works when:** You need to transition from downtime into action, or the party just arrived somewhere new and you want to establish normalcy before breaking it.

**Example:** *"The coffee is good here. The first good cup in two weeks. The sailor beside you is telling a story about a whale. Then the harbor bell starts ringing — not the hour bell, the other one. The one they ring for fires. And for ships that aren't supposed to be here."*

**Failure mode:** If the interruption doesn't connect to an open thread, it feels random. The knock on the door must be from someone the party recognizes or fears, not a stranger with a quest.

### 3. The Standoff — Already Committed

The party arrives and finds two forces already in tension. They're the variable. Both sides want them, or neither side trusts them, or their mere presence destabilizes the balance.

**Works when:** Faction conflicts are live, the party is known in the area, or you want to force a choice early.

**Example:** *"The market is too quiet. Half the stalls are shuttered. At the north end, six soldiers in red — the Ironmantle garrison. At the south end, four dockhands with crowbars, standing in front of a warehouse door. Both groups are looking at you."*

**Failure mode:** If the party has no reason to care about either side, they'll shrug and leave. At least one side must have a personal connection to a PC. A standoff between strangers is scenery.

### 4. The Discovery — Already Wrong

The party finds something that shouldn't be there, or finds something missing that should be. The discovery itself is the hook — they're now the ones who know, and knowing creates obligation or danger.

**Works when:** You're revealing a conspiracy, advancing a mystery, or showing the consequences of something the party did (or didn't do) last session.

**Example:** *"The ship is exactly where you left it. The crew is not. The gangplank is down. The cargo hold is open. On the deck, in chalk, someone has drawn the same symbol you found in the temple three days ago."*

**Failure mode:** If the discovery requires explanation, it's not a discovery — it's exposition. The wrongness must be visceral and immediate. "The door is open and there's blood" works. "You notice the sigils have been rearranged according to the Third Protocol" does not.

### 5. The Return — It's Not Over

Something the party thought they resolved has come back, changed. The villain they spared, the deal they made, the ally they left behind. The message is clear: choices have consequences, and the world doesn't stay fixed.

**Works when:** You want to show the world reacting to the party's choices, or you need to reintroduce a thread that went cold.

**Example:** *"You recognize the voice before you see the face. It's the merchant from Kalowe — the one who swore he'd never leave the island. He's standing in the road ahead of you, and he's not alone. And he's not smiling the way he used to."*

**Failure mode:** If the callback doesn't relate to a choice the party made, it's just a returning character — not a "Return" start. The power of this type is showing that the party's agency shaped the world. Without that, it's just "hey, remember this NPC?"

## Strong Start Quality Checklist

- [ ] Can you describe the situation in one sentence without referencing lore?
- [ ] Does the party need to make a choice within 30 seconds of the narration ending?
- [ ] Does it connect to at least one open thread from `wiki/hot.md`?
- [ ] Is the first line read-aloud ready (Mercer-voice, sensory, present tense, second person)?
- [ ] Would the situation still make sense if the party forgot everything from last session?

If any answer is "no," rework the start.

## Strong Start Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **The Recap Start** | "Last time, you..." puts players in listening mode, not acting mode | Open mid-action. The recap is implicit in what's happening. |
| **The Lore Gate** | Situation requires knowledge the players might not remember | Make the pressure physical and immediate. Context can come later through play. |
| **The Gentle Nudge** | "A messenger arrives with a letter..." is passive; party is receiving, not doing | The messenger is bleeding. The letter is on fire. Make arrival an event, not a delivery. |
| **The Split Party Opener** | Opening in a scene that only involves 2 of 5 players | Open with the full party present, or at minimum the majority. Split after, not before. |
| **The Consequence-Free Hook** | "You hear a rumor about..." — party can just ignore it | Make ignoring it visibly costly. The rumor walks through the door. |
| **The Over-Authored Start** | 8 paragraphs of read-aloud before the party can act | 2–4 sentences max. Every sentence after the fourth is the GM talking to themselves. |
