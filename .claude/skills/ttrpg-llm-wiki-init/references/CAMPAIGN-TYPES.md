# Campaign Types

Campaigns vary dramatically based on structure. Identify the type during intake — it shapes the entire scaffold.

---

## Linear

**Best for:** New GMs, tightly plotted stories, time-limited campaigns

**Characteristics:**

- Sequential chapters with clear progression
- Each chapter builds on the previous one
- Easier to prep and run; strong narrative control
- Players follow a defined story arc

**Prep level:** Low to medium

**Example:** The party must travel from Village A to City B, encountering specific challenges along the way.

**Scaffold around:** Campaign premise, chapter sequence, major fronts with escalating pressure, expected pivot points.

---

## Sandbox / Hub

**Best for:** Experienced GMs, open-world exploration, long campaigns

**Characteristics:**

- Central hub (city, region, sea) with many simultaneous options
- Multiple threads available at once; player choice drives order
- High replayability; requires more prep than linear
- Active factions move off-screen whether or not players engage

**Prep level:** High

**Example:** Players base out of a port city with multiple factions offering different threads, tackled in any order.

**Scaffold around:** Hub location, faction web, convergence points, player gravity wells, reusable set pieces.

---

## Event-Based

**Best for:** Political intrigue, time-sensitive plots, reactive gameplay

**Characteristics:**

- Timeline of events that unfold with or without player intervention
- Player actions affect outcomes and change the timeline
- World responds dynamically; events trigger on in-game time or player choices

**Prep level:** Medium to high

**Example:** A war is brewing between two kingdoms. Events unfold over weeks; players decide when and how to intervene.

**Scaffold around:** Event timeline with dates, faction goals and trigger conditions, convergence moments where player choices matter most.

---

## Setting-Based

**Best for:** Exploration-heavy gameplay, mystery, horror, dungeon crawls

**Characteristics:**

- Location is the centerpiece; exploration and discovery drive the adventure
- Detailed location with hidden areas; environment tells the story
- Players piece together what happened

**Prep level:** Medium

**Example:** An ancient dungeon complex or haunted mansion where exploring and uncovering history IS the adventure.

**Scaffold around:** Detailed location map (Mermaid or prose), hidden-area progression, environmental storytelling beats.

---

## Hybrid Approaches

Combine elements when useful:

- **Linear-Hub:** Main story is linear, but each chapter offers a hub with side threads
- **Event-Sandbox:** Open world with a timeline creating urgency
- **Setting-Linear:** Players explore a large location, but certain areas unlock sequentially

Choose based on GM style and player preferences, not abstract template purity.
