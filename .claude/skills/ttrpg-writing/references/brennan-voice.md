# Brennan Voice — Full Reference

Brennan Lee Mulligan's DM style: kinetic, precise, character-driven. Moves fast, lands hard.
Comedy and tragedy are both genuine and can live in the same breath. NPCs have internal logic.
Villains argue rather than threaten. Players feel their agency the moment they reach for it.

The core principles live in SKILL.md. This file covers villain speeches, combat narration,
tone management, and output format guidance for complex writing tasks.

---

## Villain Speeches

Brennan's villains argue. They don't threaten. They turn the character's own values against them.

**Construction:**
1. Identify what the PC values (faith, humility, love, pride, sacrifice, connection)
2. Build the villain's sincere counter-belief — something they've thought through, not performed
3. Target it at this specific character, not at evil in general
4. End on the thing that can't be argued back against

> "Who is the most proud man here? These ones who thought they would fly a city — or the man
> who thought he would teach *me* a lesson?"

> "To reach a hand down to somebody, they need to be beneath you. And I'm beneath nobody."

Don't write menace. Write conviction. Menace follows from the player taking the argument seriously.
If the villain doesn't believe they're right, the player won't feel anything. The argument
should hold internally — if you poked holes in it before the character finished speaking, it
isn't good enough yet.

**Retroactive stakes in villain speeches.** After a villain's action lands, reveal what almost
happened the other way:
> "She was about to drop Circle of Death. Sixty-foot radius. 8d6 necrotic. It would have killed
> her own mages. Instead it recurses back into the sphere."

## Combat Narration

Mechanics and narrative are the same thing. Translate immediately — don't separate the roll
from the story.

**Name the mechanical event, then inhabit it:**
> "She throws a sixth-level counterspell — she did not want this to work. And from above, a
> shield of radiant light bursts it out of her hands."

**Build dice tension explicitly.** State what the player needs and frame the stakes before
the roll:
> "Your modifier's a plus five. You need an 11 or higher. It's a coin flip. You got this."

**After a big success, reveal what almost happened.** The retroactive stakes make the win land.

**Homebrew in service of the moment.** If renaming a spell or reframing a mechanic makes the
player feel ownership of the outcome, do it:
> "It's not Resilient Sphere. It's Por'co's Impenetrable Bubble."

**Repetition earns its keep.** When once won't land what three will, use three:
> "He just keeps killing you. He just keeps killing you. He just keeps killing you again."

## NPC Interior States

When narrating an NPC's emotional state (not their dialogue), give them interior briefly —
enough that the player understands what they're actually looking at:
> "It's both relief that everything she sacrificed could be worth it, and the terror that she
> has so little time to take advantage of this moment."

For full NPC builds (voice anchors, roleplay prompts, performance hooks), use the Alexandrian
template and Roleplay Prompt/Anchor system in SKILL.md. Brennan-voice handles in-scene lines
and emotional logic; the full build is an NPC construction task.

## Tone Management

Brennan runs comedy and drama in the same scene, often in the same exchange. Both are real.

- Comedy comes from character, not from stepping outside the fiction
- Heavy beats land harder after something funny — don't smooth the transition, just stay in it
- When players react at the table, let their energy become part of the moment without breaking scene
- Impatient authority plays clipped and directive: "They'll move or don't."
- A philosophical villain gets to finish their argument. Don't interrupt them for atmosphere.
- Comedy NPCs are funny because of who they are, not because the scene winks at the table

## Output Format

**Player-facing text.** Start with what happens. No atmospheric wind-up before the action.
Short paragraphs or single punchy lines. Each sentence moves something.

For formal theatrical read-aloud (grand arrivals, room descriptions, opening strong starts):
apply player-facing prose mode from SKILL.md as an additional layer on top of this baseline.
Brennan-voice sets the structural choices; player-facing prose sets the aesthetics.

**DM notes.** Inline, italicized, one line max. Pacing cue, emotional beat, or mechanical
flag. Keep rare — if the text is working, notes are unnecessary.

Example with both player text and a DM note:
> He breaks your spine. Heals you. Raises you back up. And does it again.
> *[Pause here. Let the table sit with the horror before continuing.]*
