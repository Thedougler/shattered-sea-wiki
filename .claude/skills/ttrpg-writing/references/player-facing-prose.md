# Player-Facing Prose

Player-facing text is what the players experience as the world. It goes in read-aloud
callouts, handouts, in-world documents, and session recaps. The DM speaks this text aloud
or hands it to players directly.

The goal is immediate immersion: players picture the scene, feel the stakes, and want to
engage — without the DM having to do creative work in the moment.

---

## Core Principles

### Second-Person Present Tense
For read-aloud text, the player *is* the camera. This is the only context where second
person is appropriate in this campaign. Third-person objective is for DM notes. Second-
person present is for what players hear.

```
✅ You push open the door and cold air rolls over you.
❌ The party entered the room and felt cold air.
```

For session recaps, use third-person past tense — these are narrated history, not
present-tense experience.

### The Slow Zoom
Begin broad (the overall atmosphere, the feeling of the space) then zoom inward to
specific, telling details. Never dump all details at once. Build toward the point the
players need to act on.

```
The cavern opens before you, vast and cathedral-dark. The drip of water echoes somewhere 
deep within, each drop swallowed by silence before the next one falls. And there, half-
consumed by shadow at the far end, something waits.
```

### Sensory Layering
Strong read-aloud hits at least three senses. Visual alone is weak.

| Sense | Examples |
|---|---|
| Sound | echoes, silence, distant sounds, breathing, wind, dripping |
| Smell | rot, incense, salt, iron, rain on stone, char |
| Touch/temperature | cold air, rough stone, heat, damp, slick surfaces |
| Taste | (sparingly) blood, smoke, brine |

### The Active Environment
The world does things. Torchlight doesn't just exist — it "flickers and dances". Shadows
"pool in the corners". Stone walls are "slick with centuries of moisture."

Strong verbs: flickers, dances, coils, pools, drifts, presses, clings, hangs, breathes.

### Earned Specificity
Specific detail beats vague mood words. One good specific detail is stronger than three
generic ones.

```
❌ an old wooden door
✅ a door of weathered oak, its iron bands gone orange with rust, the wood dark 
   where water has worked at it for years
```

Only add detail the source material or brief supports. Never invent lore, stakes, or
sensory facts not present in the DM notes.

### The Trailing Hook
End read-aloud descriptions on something unresolved: a sound, a shadow, a door ajar, a
figure seen at the edge of vision. Leave the players leaning forward.

### Rhythm Through Punctuation
Use commas and short sentences to control pacing. A short sentence after a long one lands
like a beat drop.

```
The fire in the hearth is warm, the food on the table still steaming. The front door is 
wide open.
```

---

## The Sandbox Rule (Critical)

The Shattered Sea is a sandbox. Read-aloud text must describe the world — it cannot direct
the characters. End every sentence with the world doing something, never with a PC taking
an action, deciding something, or feeling something.

```
❌ Your breath catches as the ship crests the wave.
✅ The ship crests the wave, and for one suspended moment the whole sea is below you.

❌ You feel the weight of what you've done.
✅ The square is empty now. The torch they lit is still burning.

❌ Something tells you this is a trap.
✅ The lock is too clean. The dust on the floor stops exactly at the threshold.
```

## Content Types

### Room / Location Descriptions
**Structure**: Atmospheric establishing beat → zoom to 2–3 key details (3 senses minimum)
→ trailing hook or invitation to act.

**Length**:
- Full: 3–5 sentences. Rich but focused. Every sentence earns its place.
- Lite: 2–3 sentences. Same rhythm, tighter. For transition rooms or repeated entries.

**Example** (ruined temple, cultist occupation):

> The temple has not been kind to time, and time has not been kind to it. Pillars list at
> precarious angles, their carvings worn smooth by rain that found its way through the
> shattered roof above. The air is heavy — incense and something older beneath it, less
> pleasant. Candlelight blooms in the darkness ahead, a dozen flames clustered around an
> altar that was not built for whatever now rests upon it. And from somewhere deeper in the
> ruin, low voices chant in a rhythm that feels wrong, like a song you almost recognize.

### NPC Introductions
**Structure**: Physical presence (size, posture, how they occupy space) → one distinctive
detail → manner or energy → first words or action.

One memorable detail is more effective than a description inventory. It crystallizes the
whole person in a single image.

**Example** (grizzled dwarven blacksmith, suspicious of strangers):

> The dwarf behind the counter doesn't look up when you enter. She's bent over something at
> the anvil, turning it in her hands with the deliberateness of someone who has spent fifty
> years learning that patience produces better work than haste. She's broad even for a dwarf,
> her forearms ropy with muscle, her grey hair pulled back severely. A scar runs from her
> left temple to her jaw, old and silver. When she finally looks at you, her eyes are the
> colour of forge smoke — and just as unreadable.
>
> "Door was unlocked," she says. "Doesn't mean you were invited."

### Combat Narration
**Structure**: Action → impact → result → return agency to players.

Kinetic and immediate. Honor the mechanics while making them feel real. Avoid gore for its
own sake. The power is in momentum and consequence.

**Player lands a hit**:
> The blade finds its mark — a flash of contact at the creature's flank, and it staggers,
> a sound leaving it that is more surprise than pain. Then the fury floods back in.

**Enemy hits a player**:
> The impact is enormous, more force than you were ready for. The world tilts. You find the
> ground and have to consciously remind yourself that the fight isn't over.

**Killing blow**:
> And then it's still. The thing that moments ago seemed impossible to stop lies crumpled at
> your feet, and the silence that follows is almost louder than the fight was.

## What to Avoid

- **Em dashes**: Never use —. Use a short sentence instead to land the beat.
- **Passive voice**: "A door can be seen" → "A door stands before you."
- **Generic fantasy filler**: "ancient and mysterious," "filled with darkness," "of unknown origin."
- **Player interiority**: Don't write what characters decide, feel, or fear — describe what
  the world does that might produce those responses.
- **Over-explanation**: Trust the sensory detail. Don't explain what it means emotionally.
- **Purple prose**: Rich is not the same as overwrought. If a sentence doesn't earn its
  length, cut it.

## Quick Reference: Strong Sentence Starters

```
"The [noun] [active verb]s around you..."
"As you [action], you notice..."
"There is a [quality] to this place — [elaboration]."
"The air here [active verb]s with [sensory detail]."
"[Broad atmospheric beat]. And then — [specific detail]."
"[NPC name] doesn't [expected action]. Instead, [distinctive behavior]."
```
