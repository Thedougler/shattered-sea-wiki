# Aldenmere

## Overview

Aldenmere is the fourth island of the [[content/shattered-sea/places/crown-islands/index|Crown Islands]], one step east of [[content/shattered-sea/places/crown-islands/Harwick|Harwick]] and one step west of [[content/shattered-sea/places/crown-islands/Stenmark|Stenmark]]. There is no fortress here. No admiralty office, no naval yard, no inspection pier. The [[Dravosi-Crown|Dravosi Crown]] holds the island on paper and sends a revenue officer twice a year with a ledger and expectations about the fishing tithe. The island pays and the officer leaves. That is the full extent of colonial administration on Aldenmere.

The name comes from the mere — a broad, still tidal lake in the island's interior, connected to the sea by a narrow inlet channel. The farms run down to its eastern shore. The fishing fleets work both the mere and the offshore grounds. The town grew up where the ground was solid enough for stone, and has not grown much since. ^[inferred]

## Geography

The island is lower than [[content/shattered-sea/places/crown-islands/Harwick|Harwick]] and greener than [[content/shattered-sea/places/calven/calven-summary|Calven]] — rolling agricultural land, hedgerow-bordered fields, and a central depression that floods with the tides and never fully drains. That depression is the mere. It is perhaps a mile across at its widest, shallower in summer and deeper in spring, ringed by reed beds and low pasture and old alder stands that give some accounts of the name a second reading.

The coast is mostly gentle: short cliffs on the northern face taking the open ocean, shelving beaches and tidal flat on the south. The harbour is on the southwestern side, sheltered by a natural headland from the worst of the prevailing winds. The approaches are straightforward enough that a visiting captain does not strictly need a local pilot, though local pilots will tell you otherwise. ^[inferred]

## The Mere

The inland lake is the island's defining feature and its oldest source of protein. The mere produces freshwater eels, crayfish, and reed-filtered fish in quantities that the fishing families harvest on a seasonal rotation established well before the Crown arrived to take a cut of it. The inlet channel is navigable by flat-bottomed boats at high tide, which gives the mere a working function as a sheltered anchorage for small craft in bad weather.

The deeper sections of the mere stay murky year-round. Locals know which channels are safe and which are not, and they do not always explain the difference to visitors. The hazards are not subtle once encountered, but the encounter is usually the first warning. ^[inferred]

## The Town

The main settlement, also called Aldenmere, runs along the eastern shore of the mere and back up the slope toward the harbour road. Stone houses, low and practical. A market square that doubles as a fish-drying ground in season. A harbourmaster's office that is genuinely just an office — no rating station, no Crown registry desk, no inspection tier. Ships stop here for water, provisions, and occasionally shelter, pay a modest berthing fee, and move on. The town does not encourage long stays, not out of hostility but out of a settled sense that nothing here requires one.

The fishing fleet is the island's real economy. Two dozen boats working the offshore grounds and the northern upwelling, a smaller number working the mere year-round. The catch goes to Harwick and Tidefall by the regular provisioning routes. ^[inferred]

## Roads & Routes

Ships use the southwestern harbour or the sheltered headland. Flat-bottomed boats can work the inlet into the mere at high tide, while roads connect the harbour, town, farms, and lake shore.

## People & Power

The island pays its tithe and is otherwise governed by local habit, fishing families, and practical harbour officials. Crown administration appears as a revenue officer twice a year.

## Trade & Resources

Aldenmere sends fish, preserved catch, eels, crayfish, grain, root vegetables, orchard fruit, reeds, and provisions toward Harwick and Tidefall.

## Flora

The farmland runs to grain and root vegetables on the upper slopes, and to pasture and orchard on the lower ground facing the mere. Alder grows dense around the waterline — old stands with roots in the water, branches arching low. The reed beds on the mere's northern and western margins are extensive: tall common reed, sedge, and bulrush, cut annually by the island's thatch workers and left to regrow. The coastal scrub on the northern cliffs holds gorse, sea campion, and wind-beaten hawthorn. Below the harbour waterline, sea-grass runs in wide meadows through the clear shallows. ^[inferred]

## Fauna

The mere holds [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-frog|giant frogs]] in the reed beds and shallower margins — a seasonal nuisance for the livestock grazing nearby, a perennial hazard for anyone wading the channels at dusk. [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-toad|Giant toads]] occupy the deeper waterline, slower and harder to spot against the mud. The more serious concern is the [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-crocodile|giant crocodile]] population in the inland inlet channel and the deeper mere sections: the island keeps a culling record, but the population is old and the mere is large and the record is not always current. Locals do not swim in the mere. They do not explain this to visitors in advance.

Offshore, the southern approaches carry [[hunter-shark|hunter sharks]] as a matter of course. The northern cliff base produces [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-crab|giant crabs]] in the rock pools and kelp tangles. [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-owl|Giant owls]] nest in the oldest alder stands, hunting the farmland at night. They are considered good luck by the island's farmers, who hold the opinion partly on the basis of the owls eating smaller things that would otherwise eat the grain. ^[inferred]

## Hazards

The mere's deeper channels, giant frogs, giant toads, giant crocodiles, hunter sharks, and understated local knowledge are the main dangers.

## Current Hooks

- Visitors are not always told which mere channels are unsafe.
- The fishing tithe can become a pressure point when the Crown wants more than the island usually pays.
