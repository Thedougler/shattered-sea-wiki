# The Central Strait

![[Central-Strait.webp|Wide blue-green shipping strait cutting between low islands and reef shallows]]

## Overview

The Central Strait is the main east-west highway of the Scatter — a broad corridor of pale blue-green water running forty to eighty miles wide between the [[Crown-Islands|Crown Islands]] to the north and the [[Midchain|Midchain]] to the south. The water is clear enough that the sandy bottom shows through on good days, though the sounding line will tell you it is eighty to two hundred feet down and the bottom has no opinions about your draft. The Scatter Current pushes steadily east through the whole length of it, giving eastbound ships half a knot to a full knot they did not have to earn. Pods of spinner dolphins work the shipping lanes year-round, and flying fish break silver off every bow from the western mouth to the Narrows.

Deep water, sheltered approaches, and a current behind you: three things that rarely arrive together. Almost everything moving between the colonial homelands and [[Midchain]] uses the Strait. The cost of that convenience is paid at the western end, where [[Port-Tidefall|Port Tidefall]] extends its inspection pier across the channel and nothing enters under Crown law without pausing to answer questions and sign things.

Eastbound, the Strait narrows as both arcs converge toward [[Tail|the Tail]]. The current accelerates. The water changes character. By the time [[Stenmark|Stenmark]]'s cliffs are visible to the north, the comfortable certainties of the mid-Strait are already behind you.

## Geography

At its widest — roughly south of [[Harwick|Harwick]] — the Strait runs close to eighty miles across. At the western mouth it compresses to forty miles between [[Calders-Tooth-Overview|Calder's Tooth]] and the nearest Midchain approaches. At the eastern end, where the arcs pinch toward [[Tail|the Tail]], it narrows further and the sounding depths increase sharply as the shelf gives way to the deep water that eventually becomes the [[Drowned-Maw|Drowned Maw]].

The prevailing winds blow northeast to southwest across the whole Scatter. Eastbound ships run with them and the current both; the Strait is generous in that direction. Westbound ships beat against the wind for the full length of the passage, tacking between the northern and southern shore, which is how experienced pilots develop opinions about which tacks are less bad. The broad mid-Strait is the worst of it. Closer to the Midchain's northern reef faces or [[Crown-Islands|the Crown Islands]]' southern coasts there are lee pockets worth knowing.

Fog forms in the eastern Strait overnight and burns off by mid-morning, more reliably in summer than in winter. The western mouth stays clearer. Rain squalls move through fast and without much warning; the spinner dolphins tend to sound before them, which is the kind of navigation aid that does not appear on any chart.

## The Scatter Current

The Scatter Current is the defining fact of Strait navigation. It runs east through the full length of the passage, strongest in mid-channel and in the deeper sections of the Blue Lane, weakest close to the northern and southern reef approaches where bottom friction slows it. A ship crossing the current at the wrong angle loses way noticeably. Every experienced captain knows where the current runs hard and plans accordingly.

The current turns north at the eastern end, bending away from the Strait and up along the Maw's western edge rather than running out into open water. Eastbound ships that miss the turn ride it north instead of through — a navigational error with consequences ranging from inconvenient to serious depending on how close they are to [[Stenmark|Stenmark]]'s cliffs when they realise it. That bend in the current is called the Cold Turn by pilots who have run it and the Fool's Assist by pilots who have watched others miss it. ^[inferred]

## Routes & Access

**The Tidefall Gate** — The western entrance proper: the navigable water between [[Calders-Tooth-Overview|Calder's Tooth]]'s eastern shore and the northernmost Midchain reefs. Forty miles across on a clear day with the Crown's inspection pier visible from the south approach. Entering the Gate means entering Crown jurisdiction. Ships that try to skirt the northern edge and slip past Tidefall without presenting papers generally find that the [[Dravosi-Crown|Crown]] patrols know exactly which reef gap they are using and have formed an opinion about it. ^[inferred]

**The Blue Lane** — The established mid-Strait shipping route, following the strongest line of the Scatter Current roughly equidistant between the northern and southern shores. It is not a marked channel. It is where every merchant goes because that is where every merchant went before them. The water is deepest here, the current is strongest, and a ship in the Blue Lane is visible to both shores, which cuts both ways: Crown patrols can see you, but so can anyone else watching traffic.

**The Calven Roads** — The broad, sheltered water south of [[content/shattered-sea/places/calven/index|Calven]] island, between the Calven coast and the mid-Strait. The prevailing wind is partially blocked by the island mass, which makes this one of the better anchorage options in the northern Strait for ships waiting out weather or waiting for tide. [[Calveno|Calveno]]'s canal city is visible from here on clear days — pale stone rising four stories above the waterline, close enough that a spyglass picks up individual boats in the channels. ^[inferred]

**The Harwick Reach** — The deeper water south of [[Harwick|Harwick]], where the island's deep-water anchorage faces onto the Strait. Crown patrol frigates and enforcement vessels stage from here. A ship on the Blue Lane can see the masts of the Harwick Naval Yard from mid-channel in calm conditions. This is also where Crown convoy escorts form up before running east or west with cargo. Captains who carry their papers in good order find the Reach uneventful. Others find it less so.

**Sandtable Shoal** — A mid-Strait sandbank south of [[Aldenmere|Aldenmere]], where the bottom rises from two hundred feet to less than forty over a distance of roughly two miles before dropping back again. It is charted, surveyed, and well-marked on Crown charts, which does not prevent a reliable number of ships from finding it anyway, usually at night. The shoal deflects the Blue Lane slightly south before it recovers its line; this is the accepted route. Ships that hold the direct line to save distance mostly stay on the shoal long enough to regret it. The Crown salvage contract for Sandtable groundings is currently held by a [[Kalowe|Kalowe]] operator, which the Harwick Admiralty considers an embarrassment and has not yet resolved. ^[inferred]

**The Kalowe Run** — The angled approach from the Blue Lane south to [[Kalowe|Kalowe]], cutting through the northern Midchain reef approaches. Not a formal channel; more a set of agreed waypoints that pilots pass mouth to ear rather than chart to chart. Ships dropping off the Blue Lane for Kalowe typically turn south between Sandtable Shoal and the Harwick Reach, which gives them a clean line into the Midchain approaches without crossing into the heaviest Crown patrol water. Smugglers and registered merchants both use the Run. The difference is in which part of the approach they take and how much their papers cost.

**The Spillback** — Where the Greyteeth reef water from the eastern Crown Islands bleeds south into the Strait approaches. The Greyteeth reefs between Crown Islands run north-south; in the eastern Strait, their outrider formations extend south of the island chain and into shipping lanes that charts assume are clear. Currents here are unpredictable, depths change without apology, and the bottom is hard limestone rather than the forgiving sand of the mid-Strait. Pilots who work the eastern Strait earn their premium specifically because of the Spillback. Ships that skip the pilot on a passage this far east are the ones that generate salvage work. ^[inferred]

**The Narrows** — The eastern compression of the Strait, where both island arcs close in toward the Tail and the corridor width drops to under twenty miles at the tightest point. The current runs fast here. Fog hangs later in the morning than it does in the mid-Strait and comes back earlier in the evening. The water changes colour as the Maw's influence begins — not the dramatic discolouration visible from Stenmark's eastern cliffs, but something subtler: the blue-green going a shade deeper, the bottom no longer visible even on clear days. Pilots describe it as the water starting to think about something. ^[inferred]

## Landmarks

A sailor running the Strait east has a sequence of landmarks to fix position against. West to east:

**Fort Crestwall** — [[Fort-Crestwall|The fortified crown]] above [[Port-Tidefall|Port Tidefall]], visible from the western approach and south into the Strait on clear days. Arriving captains use it to orient before [[Inspection-Pier]] comes into view. Departing captains watch it shrink behind them as a rough measure of how far they are from Crown authority and how close they are to Crown authority catching up.

**The Pale City** — Calveno visible from mid-Strait: pale stone facades catching morning light, the city's height above the waterline making it legible at distances where most settlements are invisible. Experienced pilots use the angle of the city against the horizon to confirm their northing.

**The Yard Masts** — The standing masts and shipway cranes of the Harwick Naval Yard, visible from mid-channel when the weather cooperates. A useful mid-passage bearing. Notably, when there are no masts moving in the Yard — no ships in construction, no fitting-out work underway — captains who have run this passage often remark on it, without being fully able to say why it unsettles them.

**Aldenmere Gap** — Not a named passage but a navigational reference: the visible open water between Aldenmere's western headland and Harwick's eastern approach, which pilots use to confirm their position in the eastern mid-Strait before committing to the Blue Lane's run toward the Spillback. ^[inferred]

**The Eyrie Light** — The [[High-Eyrie|High Eyrie]] sea stack is visible from the eastern Strait on clear days: a dark vertical mark on the northeastern horizon, distinct from the lower island profiles. Pilots call it the Eyrie Light not because it carries a light but because spotting it means you are positioned correctly to avoid the worst of the Spillback and run clean into the Narrows. ^[inferred]

**Stenmark Tower** — The Crown signal station on Stenmark's eastern cliff edge is the last charted landmark before the Narrows close fully and the Tail begins. Its position above the cliffs makes it visible from the Strait floor. A signal flag on the tower means the Maw is behaving unusually. No flag means conditions are normal, which experienced captains have learned means conditions are merely not unusual enough to mention.

## Hazards

Most Strait danger is routine enough to become background noise. [[reef-shark|Reef sharks]] work the shallows and the reef approaches on both shores. [[hunter-shark|Hunter sharks]] range through the deeper lanes, particularly along the Harwick Reach where Yard waste enters the water. The occasional [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-octopus|giant octopus]] occupies wreckage around Sandtable Shoal or the Spillback limestone, which is annoying primarily for divers running salvage but occasionally relevant to moored vessels.

The exception sailors talk around rather than directly is the [[Sawek|Sawek]], a blue-hole predator associated with the darker channels in the southern Midchain approaches. Its range in the open Strait is unclear. Pilots do not treat deep, still water in the Strait as empty water, particularly in the sections of the Blue Lane where the bottom drops sharply and the current carries warm surface water over cold dark water below.

## People & Power

The Strait is nominally a Crown-controlled corridor because the western gate, inspection regime, convoy practice, and northern harbours all favour Dravosi administration. In practice, the Strait belongs to whoever can read the current, afford the papers, pick the right pilot, and avoid being noticed at the wrong time. Kalowe operators, Midchain smugglers, Crown patrols, Tidefall officials, and Harwick naval interests all shape passage.

## Trade & Resources

Deep water, sheltered approaches, and the east-running Scatter Current make the Strait the main trade artery of the region. It carries colonial cargo, inspected shipping, free-port traffic, salvage contracts, convoy escorts, Tidefall fees, Kalowe repair traffic, and enough fishing activity to feed predators along both shores.

## Flora

No distinct Strait flora is documented. The region is defined by open water, reef edges, current lines, shoals, and the island coasts that bound it.

## Piracy

Piracy in the Strait usually arrives wearing paperwork before it raises a flag. Small crews and sharper [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/humanoid/pirate-captain|captains]] work the Tidefall Gate approaches, where ships have slowed for inspection or are still settling their trim after paying for it. The Kalowe Run's southern approaches are less watched. Ships that come off the Blue Lane early or run close to the Midchain reef faces are further from Crown patrols than they sometimes realise.

The [[The-Bad-Receipt|Bad Receipt]] is the name merchants lower their voices for along the southern approach: a Kalowe-refit schooner with enough false registry to make a lawful captain hesitate before deciding it is not a legitimate vessel. By the time the decision is made, the closing speed has already narrowed the options. Larger fears belong farther east. If the [[The-Glass-Debt|Glass Debt]] or [[The-Velvet-Noose|Velvet Noose]] is reported anywhere near the Strait, convoys consolidate, Crown patrols stop pretending the schedule is normal, and people start using the word [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/humanoid/pirate-admiral|admiral]] where they would usually say captain.

## Fauna

The Strait's surface is genuinely alive in ways that make it easy to forget what lives below it. Spinner dolphins run the shipping lanes year-round, riding bow waves and working the current edges for fish. Flying fish breach silver off every bow from the western mouth to the Narrows. The visible biology of the Strait is pleasant and unremarkable.

[[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/sea-life/hunter-shark|Hunter sharks]] range through the deeper lanes with less regard for the pleasant surface picture. The Harwick Reach is the worst concentration — Yard waste in the water, fish offal from the provisioning wharves, a food chain that starts at the surface and pulls predators up. Wrecks and reef approaches along both shores attract [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-octopus|giant octopuses]] in numbers that matter primarily to salvage divers but occasionally to moored vessels that have anchored closer to limestone structure than they should have.

The [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/sea-life/Sawek|Sawek]] is the Strait's rumoured apex — a blue-hole predator associated with the darkest sections of the southern Midchain approaches, where the bottom drops fast and the current carries warm water over cold. Whether it ranges through the open Blue Lane or holds to the deep still sections is a question most pilots prefer not to resolve by experiment. The Strait's transparency deceives: two hundred feet of clear water and a sandy bottom still has things in it that the bottom does not display.
