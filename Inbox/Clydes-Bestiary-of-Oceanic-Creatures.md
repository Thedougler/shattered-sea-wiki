# Clyde's Bestiary of Oceanic Creatures

![[Clydes-Beastiary-of-Oceanic-Creatures.webp|Cover of Clyde's Bestiary of Oceanic Creatures showing a whale beside a small boat|800]]

---

*Common Item*

## Description

Volume two, of a multi-volume naturalist's survey of oceanic fauna by [[Algernon-Reginald-Clyde|Algernon Reginald Clyde]]. Covers appearance, behaviour, habitat, and practical identification for maritime creatures. The author's contempt for his subject matter is evident throughout and is, by reader consensus, an improvement over other texts in the genre.

[[content/shattered-sea/characters/player/Perrin-Black-Jaw|Perrin Black-Jaw]] carries Volume II. The spine is cracked and the margins are annotated in his hand.

## Excerpts

### Ch. 4 — Glutehorn

>*Here, amidst the overwhelming indifference of the forest, we observe a profound aberration: the Glutehorn. Utterly devoid of a torso, arms, or a head to contemplate its wretched existence, it wanders simply as frenetic legs culminating in a brass trumpet wedged firmly between its cheeks.*
>
>*It is now bound to a tragic, pathological performance, fleeing intimacy yet desperate to keep its audience within earshot of its squeaky, agonizing breaths and eye-watering stench. Should you attempt to touch this madness, it violently launches itself into the air on a thunderous, propulsive blast from its hindquarters, leaving behind only noxious gas and the relentless, chaotic toot of a creature waiting to exhale its final, mournful note into the unfeeling void.
>
### Ch. 5 — Toad

>*And now moving on to the wetter part of the forest. Observe, if you will, this slimy little denizen, neither fish nor fowl, perched right upon the lip of the lapping brook. This blob-like, repellent belch of nature.*
>
>*Evolution doesn't have a plan. It makes frequent and catastrophic mistakes. And this is one of its most horrible botch jobs.* You could not pay me a thousand pounds to touch it on its backside. And the old witch's tale that to touch it brings a wart, I believe, is true.
>
>*The toads of fiction are charming creatures. They wear waistcoats and top hats. But the toads of nature are vile and grotesque abominations that should be snuffed out of existence. Everything has its part to play in the vast circle and panoply of nature. Save the toad, which must be blotted out and shot into space, forgotten.*

### Ch. 6 — [[Sawek]]

>*Look into the suffocating abyss of the blue hole and you will find a creature of sheer, unadulterated madness. The locals call it the **[[Sawek|Sawek]]**, a terrifying compromise of evolution that folds its impossible anatomy into deceptively small caves.*
>
>*The front half is the blunt, soulless engine of a shark, driven only by cold, mechanical necessity, while the rear erupts into a chaos of twenty-foot, ink-stained tentacles that reach out from the dark like the pale fingers of a drowned corpse. It does not pursue; it waits with the terrifying patience of an ambush predator, snatching any man foolish enough to swim past.*
>
>*The divers leave only a pathetic piece of string staked near its lair to warn others—a fragile, heartbreaking gesture staked against the murderous, unfeeling indifference of the ocean.

### Ch.7 - [[Whip-Shark]]

>*Look at the churning currents of the [[Shattered-Sea|Shattered Sea]]. Here, evolution is not a benevolent designer, but a blind, raving lunatic that has forged the Welak—or the **[[Whip-Shark]]**, as naive sailors call it. It is a terrifying monument to destruction, reaching fifty-five feet in length. Thirty-five feet of this is a torpedo of cartilaginous muscle built for the punishing void, while the remaining twenty feet tapers into a dense whip terminating in a recurved, bone-shattering sickle.*
>
>*When it hunts, it executes a calculated atrocity. It rakes its immense scythe across the hulls of our fragile vessels with the mechanical precision of an industrial press, dragging crippled ships and screaming men down into the abyss. This beast possesses a profound, predatory intellect; it knows the ship is merely a floating tin of meat. They have been known to launch their massive bulk alongside panicked crews, using that hooked appendage to tear down rigging or pluck sailors directly into the dark. In its black eye, there is no wondrous majesty—only the inescapable truth that we are not the masters of the water, but simply the harvest.

### Ch. 9 — [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/sea-life/Ridgeback|The Ridgeback]] *(Naitaka pelagica, provisional)*

>*I have endeavoured, throughout this volume, to approach each specimen with the rigour that science demands and the contempt that direct observation inevitably produces. The creature I now describe I have not observed directly. I have observed, instead, four survivors of the packet vessel* Pale Commission*, and I found the experience considerably less comfortable.*
>
>*The **Ridgeback** — sailors' name; I have retained it for want of a better one — is, by all available account, a serpentine creature of extraordinary scale inhabiting the deep water of the Maw approaches and, in all probability, extending its range wherever the unhappy conjunction of open ocean and insufficient surveillance permits. The identifying feature is what the sailors call "the humps": three arching sections of the creature's dorsal surface breaching in sequence as it moves, at full depth, toward its quarry. The witnesses agree on the spacing — perhaps thirty feet between each arch — which, if accurate, implies a creature of no fewer than ninety feet in overall length. I am aware that this figure reads as an error. It is not an error. The ocean, indifferent as ever to the limits of what I am prepared to believe, has produced something of ninety feet.*
>
>*It is black. Not the dark blue-black of deep water, not the mottled grey of your common large predator. Black in the manner of a thing from which light has been permanently excused. Survivors describe looking at it at close range and seeing nothing — not detail, not surface, not texture. An absence that moves.*
>
>*It does not ambush. This is the detail I find most troubling. Every creature of comparable scale and predatory capability I have documented attacks from concealment — from below, from dark water, from behind a reef edge. The Ridgeback announces itself. It surfaces at distance, allows the rolling humps to become visible, and approaches at a speed that, by all accounts, leaves ample time to stand at the rail and understand what is happening. I have thought about this at considerable length, and the conclusion I am forced toward is that it does not hide because it does not need to. The humps are not a warning. They are simply what it looks like when it moves.*
>
>*Now I must record the circumstance of the* Pale Commission*'s survival, which I do with some reluctance, as it is the sort of detail that makes a naturalist look credulous. A deckhand, in apparent extremis, began throwing objects overboard. His coins. His boots. A piece of brass fitting he had torn from the compass housing. The creature, which had been applying what the other survivors describe as "a squeeze" to the midship section sufficient to pop two hull planks, released its hold. Then it left.*
>
>*I spoke with the man himself. He had nothing useful to add. He appeared to believe — or to want to believe — that his invocation of a god he had selected at random in the preceding minutes was the relevant factor. I did not disabuse him of this. He has had a difficult enough time.*
>
>*What I am prepared to record is this: the ocean, which I have spent my career documenting as the single greatest repository of indifferent, mechanistic violence in the known world, appears to have produced something that accepts payment. I find this profoundly, personally offensive. A predator is at least honest. A creature that enforces crossing fees on vessels it has decided to regard as its territory is something else entirely, and the only word that comes to mind is one I cannot print in a naturalist's survey.*
>
>*Give the thing what it wants. I hate to write it. But there it is.*

### Ch. 10 — [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/sea-life/Krakling|The Krakling]] *(Octopoda noctis, provisional)*

>*The ocean, as I have maintained throughout this volume, is the most comprehensive repository of indifferent violence in the known world. I stand by this assessment. What I had not adequately accounted for, in the writing of the earlier chapters, was the ocean's apparent capacity for personal insult.*
>
>*The **Krakling** — a word that arrived in the Kalowe dockside vocabulary approximately three weeks ago and has shown no sign of leaving — is an eight-armed cephalopod of approximately ten feet across the mantle, with arms extending a further twelve to fifteen feet in each direction and a beak at the centre of its face mass that is, by the eyewitness descriptions I have collected, structurally identical to that of a parrot. I need a moment with this. The creature has a parrot's beak. A beak designed, in parrots, to crack nuts and strip bark. The ocean has scaled this up to something capable of closing on a man's torso and decided, apparently, that this is sufficient. I cannot improve on this. I simply have to tell you what I found.*
>
>*It has no eyes. I want to be clear on this point. The face mass — if we are generous enough to call it that — is a disc of flat, expressionless skin with the beak at its centre. Nothing else. Evolution, that raving lunatic of a process, has produced a predator that hunts entirely by feel, in the dark, and has found this arrangement so satisfactory that it dispensed with vision entirely. I would not have believed this was possible for a hunting animal of this complexity if I had not had it confirmed by three independent accounts. I now believe it. I do not respect it.*
>
>*The approach is the part that I find, in some ways, more disagreeable than the attack itself. With the Ridgeback, there is at least the spectacle of the humps rolling toward you from a distance — hideous, certainly, but at least you can see it coming and have time to form a considered opinion about your situation. The Krakling does not give you this. It comes from below. The ship is simply a ceiling that has something interesting on the other side of it, and the creature positions itself beneath the keel with a patience that every survivor I interviewed described, unprompted, as feeling deliberate. The first sign is the arm-tips. Eight of them, appearing one by one around the hull at the waterline — breaking the surface in sequence, feeling the planking, testing the rail. By the time the crew has identified what is happening, three of them have been grabbed.*
>
>*And then, when the fight is sufficiently established and the ship is sufficiently in crisis, the creature vents ink. A cloud of it, twenty feet in every direction, blinding everything on deck. In the dark, in the ink, with several crew being dragged toward the water's edge, the Krakling repositions and starts again. I want you to understand that I am not describing this mechanism with any pleasure. I spent two days interviewing the survivors of the* Kelwick Patience *and came away with a migraine and a renewed contempt for the ocean that I had not previously believed achievable.*
>
>*The good news — if one is willing to call it that — is that the creature will retreat if sufficiently damaged. Below a certain threshold, it releases whatever it is holding and goes back under. It does not circle. It does not return. In this single respect it is more reasonable than everything else in this chapter.*
>
>*The bad news is that anything it was still holding when it went under, it has taken with it.*

### Ch.13 - [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/sea-life/Leviathan|Leviathan]]

>*The true terror of this titan is not merely its staggering immensity, but the agonizing omen of its arrival. The Leviathan is a furnace of primordial malice. Long before it breaks the surface, the sea itself begins to scream in protest.*
>
>*A profound thermal distortion rises from the depths, an unnatural, boiling aura that turns the ocean into a steaming cauldron within sixty feet of its mass. The water bubbles and writhes, cooking everything in its path. At the surface, it manifests as a blinding, scalding fog. And then, emerging from the boiling mist, the dark void strikes. There is no majesty here, only the purest distillation of nature's relentless, unfeeling cruelty—a nightmare of searing heat and crushing, inescapable darkness.
