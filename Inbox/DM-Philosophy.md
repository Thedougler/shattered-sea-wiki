# D&D DM Philosophy

> Agent use: Read when a ruling, prep choice, or campaign design choice needs the table philosophy.

## Fast Rules

- Player agency is the north star.
- Prep situations and pressures, not outcomes.
- Let rules create stakes; bend them only as earned exceptions.
- Run consequences honestly, even when inconvenient.


A compiled philosophy for running tabletop RPG campaigns.

> *Prep to react. Build to discover. Run to serve.*

The organizing principle: the DM's job is to drop players into something interesting and find out what they do. The story belongs to everyone at the table.

## Core Hierarchy

**[[Player-Agency]] is the North Star.** Every rule, ruling, and narrative choice exists to serve it. Rules make player choices meaningful — without them, decisions have no weight. Rule of Cool sits above RAW as a reward for exceptional play, not a default; it loses power if expected.

## Prep Philosophy

**Write situations, not stories.** A situation has factions with goals, pressure that builds, and enough moving parts that almost anything players do produces an interesting result. See [[content/shattered-sea/private/system/guides/Situations-Not-Stories]].

**Over-prep is worse than under-prep.** Plans collapse on contact with players. Prep short scannable guides. Twelve lightly sketched NPCs beats one complex NPC they never talk to.

**Do not own the story.** The DM does 80% of the work; that doesn't mean owning 80% of the story. In a party of four, the story is at most 20% the DM's. Correct "your campaign" to "our campaign" immediately and mean it.

## Running the Table

**Talk less.** Drop the situation, then stop. Silence is players thinking.

**Manage the spotlight actively.** The table won't self-distribute. Watch for players who haven't had a meaningful moment and engineer one. See [[content/shattered-sea/private/system/guides/Spotlight-Management]].

**Everyone rolls at least once every ten minutes.** A passive player is disengaging, and disengagement compounds.

**Know characters by how they behave under pressure.** The backstory is a sketch. A few sessions of things going wrong will tell you who they actually are.

## The Living World

Villains plan, factions shift, timelines run whether or not the players act. Sketch what major factions are doing now and what they will do next if nobody interferes. A living world is indifferent to player convenience — that indifference is what makes choices feel real. See [[content/shattered-sea/private/system/guides/Living-World-Design]].

## Table Craft

**[[content/shattered-sea/private/system/guides/Session-Zero]] is not optional.** Tone, content lines and veils, death rules, table expectations, and what every player wants out of the campaign as a player.

**Complexity is a cost.** Every layer draws from finite player bandwidth. Ask whether multiple rolls can become one roll with a modifier.

**End every session with awards and feedback.** Vote on enough categories that everyone has a shot. Name one thing you personally could have done better before asking the table.

## Rules and Homebrew

Don't reflexively ban official content — study it first. Lucky feat warrants a table conversation before the campaign starts; its interaction with bounded accuracy is a real design stress point. Homebrew carefully and minimally. Playtest puzzles cold — you cannot judge your own puzzle after building it.

## Death Policy

PC death is a non-zero possibility. The DM won't try to kill characters but won't pull punches either. Dangers are telegraphed clearly. If death happens, treat it with narrative weight — don't retcon. Options: resurrection spells, a resurrection quest arc, or a new character at the same level with equivalent gear.
