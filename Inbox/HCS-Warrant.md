# HCS Warrant

> [!READ-ALOUD]
> A Crown brigantine comes on under full sail, naval grey from waterline to rail, the white stripe catching the light in a way the patrol cutters never quite manage at this distance. Twelve gun ports run down each side, six per beam, and the brass registry plate at the bow is large enough to read before she's within hailing range. A rating officer is already at the rail with a speaking trumpet. The Crown pennant at the masthead isn't moving — she's coming downwind and making you feel it. Whatever she is, she isn't asking.
>
> ---

## Current State

The *HCS Warrant* is the Crown's primary enforcement brigantine on the Central Strait western patrol route, crewed from Harwick and working the approaches to [[content/shattered-sea/places/port-tidefall/index|Port Tidefall]]. She is larger and better armed than either of Tidefall's patrol cutters and operates under a different mandate: where the cutters run waypoints and board small traders, the *Warrant* compels compliance from vessels that the cutters couldn't safely challenge alone.

## Overview

A Tier 2 Dravosi enforcement brigantine, built at Harwick to the Admiralty's standard enforcement specification. She is armed over cargo — an armed variant at the Tier 2 scale, carrying twelve gun mounts against a reduced hold — and exists to make the argument that noncompliance is an error rather than an option. She is not fast; she doesn't need to be. A vessel that sees the *Warrant* running under Crown pennant has already calculated that running is the worse choice.

Commander Gideon Ault commands under sealed Admiralty orders. His commission authority includes vessel detention, cargo seizure, crew arrest, and the authority to issue provisional privateer letters to vessels assisting in enforcement actions.

## Layout

### Weather Deck

The main working and command space. Helm and binnacle aft, belfry and capstan amidships. The swivel guns cover the bow and stern arc; the twelve cannon below have their ports cut flush with the gun deck and run out through hinged covers that the crew drill weekly. The Crown pennant flies from the masthead in all conditions underway. An additional pennant and three spares are stored sealed in the signal locker.

### Captain's Cabin and Officer Spaces

The captain's great cabin runs the full width of the stern — private, lockable, with a fixed chart table and a separate desk for commission papers and sealed orders. The Captain's Locker is bolted to the cabin floor. A three-cabin officer suite forward of the cabin houses the Commander's watch officers. The warrant officers share a wardroom amidships with a separate chart rack and rating officer's writing desk where inspection paperwork is drafted and sealed.

### Crew Quarters and Galley

Twenty hammock berths occupy the forward lower deck. A working galley is amidships with a copper range — large enough to serve the full crew underway. A Cook's berth adjoins it.

### Below the Waterline

The magazine runs under the gun deck aft — two keys, one with Commander Ault, one sealed at Harwick Admiralty. Powder and shot are rotated on the standard Crown maintenance schedule. Forward of the magazine: the inspection bond store (sealed cargo held pending rating determination), and a prisoner hold with iron grating and six individual cages rated to Crown detention standards.

## Crew

Commander Gideon Ault commands with a complement of 24 ratings from Harwick's naval pool. His watch officers are three Patrol-Lieutenants on rotating assignment. A warrant gunner manages the cannon maintenance schedule; a warrant carpenter keeps the hull log. The crew includes two rating inspectors — warrant officers specifically trained in cargo inspection and manifest verification — who board alongside the armed party during enforcement actions.

Ault's authority extends to privateer commission. He can issue provisional letters authorising a vessel to act in Crown enforcement capacity in the Strait; these expire at the next Crown anchorage.

## Operational Notes

**Registry:** Naval grey hull, white waterline stripe, brass registry plate (bow), Crown pennant (masthead). All are removable given time and tools. The plate takes an hour with the right equipment; the stripe requires a yard. Absence of the markings in Crown waters is treated as a confession.

**Enforcement posture:** The *Warrant* does not need to fire to compel compliance. A vessel that sees twelve gun ports and a rating officer at the rail with a speaking trumpet has already made its calculation. Ault's doctrine is to position before hailing — the *Warrant* comes downwind and close before any communication is attempted.

**Boarding action:** A standard boarding from the *Warrant* sends six armed ratings under a Patrol-Lieutenant, with one rating inspector carrying the inspection manifest. The remaining crew holds the deck and the gun ports open. Ships that comply are inspected and either cleared or detained. Ships that resist face a broadside from a vessel with more than twice their gun weight.

**Privateer letters:** Ault can issue on his own commission authority. These are a standard Crown tool for recruiting temporary enforcement support in contested waters and are recorded in Harwick's admiralty ledger on the next resupply.

**Prize posture:** Detaining the *Warrant* would be noticed within a week at Harwick. The Admiralty's response is not another cutter — it is a sealed order to every Crown vessel in the Strait.
