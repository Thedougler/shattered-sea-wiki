# The High Eyrie
![[1778025863567~2.png]]
![[The-High-Eyrie.webp|Stone fortress monastery perched on a sheer sea stack above dark water]]

## Overview

The High Eyrie is a 200-foot basalt sea stack at the far northeastern edge of the [[Crown-Islands|Crown Islands]], where the northern arc narrows toward [[Tail|the Tail]] and the water starts to feel close to the [[Drowned-Maw|Drowned Maw]]. The rock rises sheer from the sea, salt-streaked from waterline to summit. There is no dock below, no real beach, and nothing on the face worth calling a handhold.

The [[Sentinels-Of-The-Eyrie|Sentinels of the Eyrie]] have lived and trained here for roughly two hundred years. They predate both Dravosi and Tessarine authority in the Scatter, and they have kept that independence. Three Dravosi administrators have tried to formalize an arrangement with the monastery. All three were politely declined.

What the Eyrie produces is record. From the summit, on a clear day, the water changes colour at the horizon where the Maw begins. The Sentinels have watched that line and everything around it for generations: compass drift, weather anomalies, surface stillness, depth sightings, wrecks, and anything else visible from circuit altitude. A Sentinel will tell you what they recorded. What it means is not a question they answer.

## Approach

From the water, the High Eyrie looks like a dark tooth driven up through the sea. The summit structures sit low and wide against the wind, built to stay put rather than impress anyone below. The only practical approach is aerial. Aarakocra land on the upper terraces with the casual precision of people who learned the local wind before they learned most social niceties.

Non-flying visitors arrive by invitation, carried harness, magic, or a very deliberate climbing operation watched from above. There is an emergency stair cut into the southern face, but it stops well above the waterline. Nobody sensible mistakes it for an entrance.

The stack's height keeps out most land predators, but the air has its own scavengers. [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/beasts/giant-vulture|Giant vultures]] ride the thermals below the monastery after storms, and Sentinel ledgers occasionally note larger shadows crossing the eastern light. Sailors call those shadows [[content/shattered-sea/beastiary/monstrosity/roc|rocs]] when they are feeling dramatic; the Sentinels only record wingspan, heading, and time.

## Description

The summit is broad enough to hold a working monastery, but never comfortable enough to forget the edge. Stone walls are low. Rooflines are blunt. Doorways face away from the worst gusts. Courtyards are open, swept clean by weather, and worn by generations of landing drills and kensei footwork.

The main hall faces east toward the Maw. The observation ledgers are kept there in bound volumes, copied and maintained in the same careful format year after year. Sleeping cells, kitchens, storage rooms, and workrooms are cut low into the stone or built along the lee side. The training terraces cantilever off the eastern face, open to the drop on three sides.

Common details:

- Constant directional wind across the summit.
- Salt in feathers, hinges, hair, and ledger bindings.
- Stone floors worn smooth by repeated drills.
- Clear east-facing sightlines, kept open by habit.
- No ornamental banners. Loose cloth is a liability here.

## Function

The High Eyrie produces record. Its main public function is observation of the Maw-facing waters, weather anomalies, compass drift, wrecks, depth sightings, and other events visible from the summit or circuit altitude.

## People & Operations

The [[Sentinels-Of-The-Eyrie|Sentinels of the Eyrie]] live, train, observe, copy ledgers, and maintain independence from colonial authority. They record what they see exactly as they see it: no conclusions, no interpretation, no editorializing.

## Access & Security

The only practical approach is aerial. Non-flying visitors arrive by invitation, carried harness, magic, or a deliberate climbing operation watched from above. The emergency stair is for rope work, rescue, and drills, not ordinary visitors.

## Public Reputation

To sailors, the High Eyrie is both landmark and warning. If you can see the stack, you are near serious water. If you can see Sentinels circling east of it, something is being recorded.

The order does not run a harbour, charge tribute, sell charts, or answer distress flags like a patrol service. Its reputation is colder and more specific: if a Sentinel says a storm line moved wrong, it moved wrong.

Researchers have sometimes been granted access to the public record, though few have the patience or context to use what they read. Visitors expecting mysticism usually find stone, ink, wind, and silence. Visitors expecting soldiers find something stranger: disciplined aerial fighters trained to observe first and interfere only when intervention preserves life without corrupting the record.

## Sentinel Customs

Sentinels are trained to record what they see, exactly as they see it. No conclusions. No interpretation. No editorializing. A sinking is logged as a sinking, with time, weather, position, water condition, and observed sequence. The ledger does not say why.

Every Sentinel also completes a pilgrimage before returning to the Eyrie as a full member of the order. The task is assigned by a master and is specific to the student: sometimes suited to a weakness, sometimes to a gift, sometimes to something the order needs observed. The task is not meant to be fair. It is meant to be exact.

## Current Hooks

- Researchers may be granted access to the public record, but the Sentinels provide observation rather than interpretation.
- A Sentinel pilgrimage task can send an aarakocra far from the Eyrie for a specific observation or correction.

## Notable Places

**The Training Terraces** — Cantilevered off the eastern face, open to the drop on three sides. New arrivals spend weeks here learning to land, move, and hold still in the peak wind.

**The Main Hall** — The east-facing hall where the observation ledgers are kept. It smells of ink, cold stone, and oil worked into old bindings.

**The Lee Cells** — Small sleeping cells built along the sheltered side of the summit, quiet except for the pressure of wind around stone corners.

**The South Stair** — An emergency stair cut into the southern face and ending above the waterline. It exists for rope work, rescue, and drills, not visitors.

**The Ledger Wall** — The interior wall of bound observation records. Visitors are watched more closely here than anywhere else in the Eyrie.
