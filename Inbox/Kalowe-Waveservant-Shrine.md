# Waveservant Shrine — Kalowe

## Overview

The Waveservant Shrine is [[Umberlee|Umberlee]]'s causeway shrine at [[Kalowe]], cut into the limestone at the main causeway junction where ships passing through the reef gap are visible from the doorway.

## Description

A stone niche in the causeway wall, covered by a low roof of salvaged timber and sealed tile. Inside: a limestone basin, a shallow ring of tallow candles, and a single [[Waveservants|Waveservant]] in salt-grey robes who has been watching ships come through the reef gap for long enough that the work has become automatic. The basin drains through a crack in the causeway stone into the lagoon. Nobody sealed it. It doesn't need sealing.

Kalowe does not have a culture of ceremony and the shrine does not pretend otherwise. You step in, you pay, you receive an acknowledgement, you leave. If you want a blessing spoken aloud, that is available. Most captains who work Kalowe regularly don't bother asking.

The gap in the reef is visible from the shrine doorway on clear mornings. That view has done more for tribute compliance than any posted doctrine.

## Function

The Waveservants operate on a tithe-and-communion model. Mariners pay before they sail or after they survive. Both are acceptable. Neither is optional. At Kalowe, a single Waveservant handles everything. There is no junior attendant. The work does not require one.

## People & Operations

One [[Waveservants|Waveservant]], rotating by assignment. Whoever holds the posting works the shrine alone — collections, candles, blessings, the brief ledger notation. The role is considered an uncomplicated assignment and given to those who will not mistake uncomplicated for unimportant.

## Access & Security

Open. The causeway junction is one of the busiest transit points in Kalowe, which gives the shrine more foot traffic than it would get anywhere quieter. Nobody leaves an offering at the basin for secrecy.

## Current Hooks

- A ship whose captain skipped tribute coming in through the reef gap will be noted. Kalowe's authority is informal, but the shrines have long memories and short ledgers.
