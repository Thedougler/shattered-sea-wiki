# The Khlysty

*"Khlysty" is an outsider name — a slur, technically, from an old word for flagellants. They do not call themselves this. Among themselves, when they use a name at all, they say "the Flock." The shepherd knows his own.*

---

## Overview

The Khlysty are a secret mystical sect threading through the noble courts of the Dravosi and Tessarine worlds. They leave no shrines, hold no charters, and carry no visible insignia. What they carry is a reputation — passed mouth to ear, in the private languages of desperate families — for curing the incurable.

A child given up by the Crown's best physicians. An heir dying of something that has no name in any medical text. A young lord whose illness has been discreetly eating the succession for two years. These families receive, through channels they couldn't fully explain afterward, a recommendation. A name. A man who travels. Who has done this before.

He arrives, and he heals them. The healing is real. It costs nothing, or almost nothing. He asks only to be a guest a while, to eat well, to move on when his business is concluded. The family remembers him fondly. They speak of him to others in their position.

The flock grows.

---

## Doctrine

The Khlysty hold that the body is not the enemy of the spirit but its instrument — that flesh taken seriously, pleasure practiced as prayer, excess pursued with genuine devotion is a path *through* the mortal condition rather than a concession to it. Sin is not the opposite of holiness. Avoidance is. The man who has never sinned has nothing to repent, and repentance is where the divine enters.

**The Turning** is their central practice: an ecstatic gathering in which participants dance, sing, fast, confess, and flagellate in sequence until the flesh is exhausted and something else takes over. Participants describe visions. Healings. The sense of being temporarily outside their own lives. Whether this is genuine mystical experience or the predictable result of sleep deprivation, pain, and collective fervor is a question the Khlysty consider irrelevant.

**The healing covenant** is implicit but real. When the Shepherd heals someone, they become part of the flock. They don't know this. No vows are asked, no signatures taken. The mechanism is Grigori's — through the act of healing, he binds a thread of his phylactery into their life. As long as they live, he cannot be permanently destroyed. He considers this a natural exchange: he gave them life, their life sustains him. The shepherd keeps the flock; the flock keeps the shepherd. He is not wrong, from where he stands.

---

## Practice

Khlysty gatherings — when they occur — are held in private houses, aboard noble vessels at anchor, or in rooms that have been locked from the inside. They are referred to as "the Turning." Participants emerge the next morning with the particular look of people who have been somewhere difficult and returned changed.

Between gatherings, the Khlysty do not congregate visibly. Members maintain their ordinary lives — court attendants, noble heirs, the occasional ship's surgeon or chaplain — and communicate through private correspondence encoded in religious language that means something different to insiders. They do not recruit. They are found by the desperate, or by those the shepherd chooses to heal.

**Flagellation** is practiced but not displayed. It is understood as physical punctuation — the body acknowledging what the spirit has moved through. Grigori has scars he does not explain. He is not embarrassed by them.

---

## Structure

The Khlysty have no visible hierarchy beyond the shepherd. Grigori is referred to by the old term for a living prophet within the sect: *Korabl* — the Ship, the vessel through which the divine navigates the mortal world. He does not use this title himself. He introduces himself as a shepherd, which he prefers. He finds "vessel" theologically sound but uncomfortable.

There is no second-in-command. If Grigori were destroyed, the sect would lose coherence — it is built around his charisma and his miracles, and nothing structural would survive him. This is a deliberate design.

---

## In the Shattered Sea

The Khlysty have no ports and no flags. They move through existing social infrastructure — the letters of introduction that flow between noble houses, the guest privileges extended to known healers, the private chapels and locked dining rooms where people of standing conduct their most discreet business.

They are not a naval power, a criminal organization, or a political faction in any formal sense. They are a network of grateful families who share a shepherd in common and do not know each other exist.

Their density is highest in the Dravosi nobility — old houses with hereditary illnesses, succession anxieties, and a cultural preference for solving problems quietly. A reasonable number of living Dravosi heirs are Grigori's phylacteries without knowing what that means.

## What the GM Should Know

The Khlysty are a late-game reveal. The party will encounter Grigori long before they understand what he is or what stands behind him. The cult should surface in layers:

1. **Early:** Grigori as a warm, enigmatic, useful traveling companion. No cult visible.
2. **Mid:** References to miraculous healings among nobility that share a description. The name "shepherd" appearing in private correspondence or old records. A noble heir who behaves strangely around Grigori when they meet — not afraid, but deferential in a way they can't account for.
3. **Late:** The phylactery network as the real shape of the threat. To destroy Grigori permanently, the party would need to sever or exhaust the network of living heirs. This is not a dungeon problem. It is a political and moral one.

**Play the cult as genuine.** The Turning is real. The healings are real. The warmth is real. The horror is that a CR 19 undead sorcerer has built an unkillable afterlife out of the gratitude of dying children and their desperate families, and considers this a reasonable thing to have done.
