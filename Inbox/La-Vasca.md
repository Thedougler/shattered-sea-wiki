# La Vasca

## Overview

The Black-Jaw Run has operated a private basin in Le Paludi for three generations. The canal approach is unremarkable — a low stone arch barely wide enough for a keel, flush with the waterline, indistinguishable from the drainage culverts on either side. On the far side of the arch, the channel widens into a covered basin cut from old foundation stone: high enough for a mast to stand, long enough for a patrol cutter or a heavy caravel. The dry dock cradle is iron and timber, hand-operated, original equipment from when the building above it was still a tanning yard. The dry dock still works. The tannery has not operated in forty years.

Outside, the building looks like what it officially is: a disused storage facility with a rotted roof and a collapsed beam visible through the upper windows. The beam is real. It is also not structural.

Within the Passage, the place is called La Vasca — the Basin. New members learn it as an address, not an explanation.

## Description

The basin smells of old stone, bilge, and the particular mineral damp of a space that has not seen direct sunlight in decades. A single oil lamp hangs on a chain over the cradle. The walls are original foundation stone — Le Paludi is built on layers of older Calveno, and the basin is somewhere in the middle stratum, stone that was old before the tannery above it existed. Rattkin-scale tool racks run along the port wall: caulking mallets, copper tacks, pitch brushes, a collection of paint pigments in sealed crocks. The starboard wall holds spare timber, a hand-pump bilge rig, and a folding worktable covered in old manifests that nobody has cleared in years.

A narrow passage in the aft wall connects to the Warren's outer channels — technically accessible on foot at low tide, not comfortable, and not discussed with anyone who hasn't already been introduced.

## People & Operations

**Cobb** — The junior Black-Jaw member who keeps the Basin running is a young Rattkin in his late teens, grey-brown fur, unremarkable except for hands that are already hardened well past his age from caulking work. He took the posting because the family needed someone reliable and he needed something to do. He is not ambitious. He does what the Basin requires: keeps the cradle maintained, manages the tool stock, escorts vouched vessels in through the arch, and sends word up the chain whenever the Basin is used.

He is not unfriendly. He will have the cradle ready before he asks any questions, and he will not ask many. What he will do — early, calmly, without making it a confrontation — is tell Perrin that Nona has been asking after him and that she will know he is here within the hour regardless. *"You should go see her before she comes to you. It'll go better."* He says this the way someone says it when they know from experience. He does not elaborate.

He has no authority to turn Perrin away. He also has no authority to let him stay in Calveno without flagging it. Those are the two facts of his position and he is comfortable with both of them.

> [!DM] Cobb is not the threat. He's the clock. Perrin using the Basin is not a secret — it is a known event being reported through proper channels to the person who runs those channels. The warmth is genuine. The reporting is also genuine. These are not in conflict. Play Cobb as someone doing his job correctly, which in this case means being helpful and honest about exactly what being helpful costs.
>
> If a player tries to ask Cobb not to tell Nona: he pauses, looks at Perrin with something between sympathy and mild disbelief, and says *"I can't do that."* Not unkind. Just: no.

## Access & Security

Canal approach only. The arch is three feet above waterline at high tide; a vessel drawing more than eight feet will need to time the approach or lighten the load. Once inside, the stone absorbs sound well enough that work in the Basin is not audible from the canal. The entrance arch looks, from the outside, like a drainage outlet.
