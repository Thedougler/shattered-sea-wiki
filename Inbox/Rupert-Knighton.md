## Overview

Rupert Knighton commands a fleet. Not a pair of patrol cutters and a manifest stamp — a fleet, Crown naval vessels, the kind that doesn't arrive for inspections. When his ships appear on the horizon, someone has already made a serious mistake.

He is not a privateer. He is not enforcement. He is the thing enforcement reports to when enforcement fails.

Within the Dravosi Crown apparatus, he operates at a level where [[Barnaby-Rook|Barnaby Rook]] was a working piece on the board, not a peer. Rook's death alone might not reach him. His son's death will.

[[Capn-Gorgeous|Cap'n Gorgeous]] was his son — or close enough to House Knighton that it amounts to the same. The party killed him in Session 01, Phase 1. Knighton doesn't know yet. When he hears, his response will not go through Crown channels. He will send his own people, who have no paperwork requirements and no interest in arrests.

Right now he doesn't know the party exists. That will change.

## The Knights

Knighton refers to every ship under his command as one of his Knights. Not by their registered hull names — those are administrative. His Knights. His fleet is his chessboard, and he moves them accordingly.

How many ships that is, exactly, is not public knowledge. Enough that it functions as a deterrent. Enough that a port captain who hears "the Knights are coming" doesn't ask follow-up questions.

---

## Manner

Posh in the old way — the kind that doesn't need to announce itself. He doesn't shout. He doesn't threaten. He gives orders, and the orders happen, and if they don't happen he replaces whoever was responsible with someone who will make them happen. His reputation is built on outcomes, not style.

He is fearsome precisely because he is not theatrical about it.

## Tactics

Every commander in the Dravosi Navy charges. Every commander flanks. Knighton does both, in sequence, in ways that make the enemy feel stupid afterward for not seeing it coming.

His signature is commitment. He commits his vanguard hard — harder than makes sense, harder than anyone believes is intentional — and the opposing line braces for impact and compresses to meet it. Then the flank arrives. Not a feint. Not a probe. The real weight, from a direction that was open thirty seconds ago and somehow isn't anymore. By the time the enemy commander understands what is happening, Knighton's ships are inside the formation.

Other fleet commanders study his engagements and come away with notes they can't use. The moves are not complicated. They are not secret. They simply require a level of coordinated nerve that most officers can't sustain under fire. Knighton can, and he knows it, and he picks engagements where that gap is the deciding factor.

He does not win every battle. He wins the ones he chooses to fight.

## Connection to Geoffrey

Geoffrey Draves is his nephew by blood. Knighton almost certainly does not think about this. Geoffrey has never met him, never received correspondence from him, and has no reason to believe the feeling is mutual. The Draves branch of the family is not the Knighton branch — it is the branch that didn't go anywhere in particular.

Whether Knighton would extend any professional or personal consideration to a nephew who has just defected to work for a foreign crew is a question that hasn't come up yet. It will be more interesting when it does.

---

## Threat Tier

Knighton is a late-campaign problem. He is placed here to exist in the background — the name that explains why Geoffrey's family has Crown access but Geoffrey himself is running cargo inspections for Rook. The gap between Geoffrey and his uncle is not just rank. It's the distance between someone the Crown uses and someone the Crown answers to.
