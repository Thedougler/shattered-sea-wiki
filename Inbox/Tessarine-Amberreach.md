# Tessarine Factor *Amberreach*

> [!READ-ALOUD]
> The ship comes in low and wide, her pale grey hull sitting deep in the water with the deliberate weight of something built to carry rather than chase. The beam is broader than a brigantine has any business being, the freeboard heavy, and the cargo ports along her sides are sealed and numbered in neat stencilled figures. A Tessarine pennant flies at the masthead — not the Crown's military pennant, just the Concordat's registry mark rendered in cloth. There are gun ports, and they are open, and no one is particularly rushing toward them. The factor's pennant below the main tells the harbour she has commercial authority aboard. The harbour knows. It was expecting her.
>
> ---

## Current State

The *Tessarine Factor Amberreach* is the Concordat's principal cargo carrier on the Scatter routes between [[content/shattered-sea/places/calveno/index]] and the western approaches, with route authority extending through the Central Strait. She runs on a fixed commercial schedule — departures logged three ports in advance, arrival windows insured through Tessarine underwriting, losses settled at ninety percent of assessed value within forty days of confirmed loss. Merchants along the route do not need to like the Concordat. They need their cargo to arrive.

Senior Factor [[Oriana-Becht]] holds authority to sign instruments aboard in the field — credit extensions up to 2,000gp, route underwriting renewals, salvage financing, and provisional letters of cargo obligation. Larger instruments go via sealed correspondence to Calveno.

## Overview

A Tier 3 Tessarine cargo galleon, built to the Concordat's standard commercial specification: maximum hold capacity, reliable long-route capability, enough armament to discourage rather than fight. She is not the largest galleon on the Scatter. She is the most predictable, and predictability at scale is a commercial weapon.

The *Amberreach* makes the argument the Concordat always makes: not that it is more powerful than the alternatives, but that refusing it makes business harder immediately while accepting it only makes business harder later. She arrives when she says she will. She carries what she agrees to carry. The contracts are clear because ambiguity is expensive.

## Layout

### Weather Deck

Open working space from bow to stern, organized around cargo logistics rather than military readiness. The helm and binnacle sit aft under a light quarterdeck canopy. No raised poop — the factor's cabin runs below the stern gallery, not above it. The cargo handling equipment dominates the main deck: davits, winches, hatch covers, and a second set of cargo booms amidships that can work two holds simultaneously in port. Forward swivel and stern swivel are mounted, manned only when the gun ports open below. The Tessarine pennant and the smaller factor's authority pennant both fly at the masthead — in most ports, the second one is the one people actually look at.

### Gun Deck and Factor Suite

The gun deck carries the sixteen cannon in pairs, eight per side, with wide spacing between mounts to allow cargo movement through the central corridor. The guns are clean, maintained, and not the point. At the stern, the gun deck opens into the factor suite: a private corridor with locked cabin doors, the sort of accommodation that implies the people inside are accustomed to a certain kind of meeting. Senior Factor Becht's cabin occupies the full starboard stern quarter — chart table, writing desk, document vault, and the Captain's Locker bolted to the sole. The factor's office is the port stern quarter: shelved contract files, correspondence drawers, a bolted meeting table, and the Chart Archive that covers the Concordat's active Scatter routes. Warrant officers and the navigator share berths forward of the suite.

### Crew and Passenger Deck

Below the gun deck: crew hammocks for sixty forward, a working galley amidships, and aft a block of six private passenger cabins rated for merchant transit — factors in transit, debtors travelling to a settlement, and occasionally people the Concordat prefers to move without using common carriers. The passenger cabins are maintained to the same standard as the factor suite: plain, functional, more expensive than they look. A surgeon's station is set into the hull amidships where the motion is least.

### Hold

The hold is the ship's argument. Four sealed cargo compartments run the length of the keel, separated by structural bulkheads and numbered in sequence. Each compartment has its own sealed manifest pinned to the hatch cover, countersigned by the loading factor, the cargo owner's representative, and the port of origin's harbour authority. One compartment is reserved for Concordat bonded cargo — goods travelling under route underwriting where the Concordat has accepted financial liability for the delivery. These hatches carry a Tessarine wax seal that is legally significant in twelve colonial ports and every Concordat house. The forward compartment is the sealed bond store: documents, letters of credit in transit, and high-value small cargo that travels separately from its manifest.

## Crew

Senior Factor Oriana Becht commands in the commercial sense — she controls the cargo, the contracts, and the ship's schedule. The Navigator holds operational authority on deck. Below them: a bosun, a carpenter, and a cook who have worked Concordat routes long enough to have opinions about which ports are worth bribing and which harbourmasters can be handled with patience and the right sealed letter.

The sixty crew are Concordat employees on route contract, not naval ratings. Competent sailors. Most of them have worked this route before. Several are former debtors who took the work as settlement. None of them were told exactly what is in the bonded compartment and none of them have asked.

## Operational Notes

**Registry:** Pale grey hull, no waterline stripe (that is a Crown mark). Tessarine pennant underway. Concordat registry mark stencilled in black on the transom and each cargo hatch cover — not brass, not decorative, entirely legible. The factor's authority pennant is the one that matters in port.

**Commercial posture:** The *Amberreach* does not need to fire to compel compliance. She compels compliance by arriving on schedule and offering the same underwriting she has offered for fifteen years. A port that refuses Tessarine cargo loses the underwriting. A port that loses the underwriting pays higher rates for the same routes through anyone else. The gun ports are open as a reminder that the Concordat has not forgotten that the world has pirates.

**Boarding action:** The *Amberreach* does not board. She is boarded — Crown rating inspections, harbour inspections, and the occasional piracy attempt that does not survive contact with sixteen cannon and sixty people who understand that their route contracts depend on the cargo arriving intact. The Concordat insures the cargo. It does not insure attackers.

**The bonded compartment:** What is in it varies by route. What is always in it is paperwork that would be interesting in the wrong hands. The sealed manifests are legally protected from Crown inspection under the commercial treaty between the Concordat and the Dravosi Admiralty, a treaty that both parties resent and neither has found a way to unilaterally dissolve.

**Route underwriting:** The *Amberreach*'s route is insured. If she sinks, the Concordat pays ninety percent of assessed cargo value within forty days. This guarantee is worth something — merchants factor it into their rates before the *Amberreach* even docks. The factor who books cargo on a competing vessel is paying an implicit premium for the risk of that vessel not being the *Amberreach*.
