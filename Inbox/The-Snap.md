# The Snap

A slim brass bracer, fitted and hinged, housing a compressed lattice of Antheri fabric folded into itself in careful origami tiers. In its resting state it reads as a finely made piece of armguard — cool to the touch, faintly iridescent where the Antheri cloth is visible through the brass housing. When activated, the clockwork mechanism fires: the lattice unfurls in a single sharp motion, snapping open into a forearm-mounted buckler, and a brief electrical charge stiffens the fabric rigid.

The whole sequence takes less than a second. The sound is a dry *crack*, like a sail catching wind.

---

## Properties

**[HB] Bracer Form.** At rest, the Fan is a slim bracer worn on the forearm. It occupies no hand.

**[HB] Fan Form.** As a **bonus action**, the attuned wearer can trigger the clockwork mechanism, unfurling the bracer into a forearm-mounted buckler. While deployed, the wearer gains **+2 AC**. This bonus applies regardless of what the wearer's hands are doing — the shield is mounted to the arm and operates independently. As a **bonus action**, the wearer can deactivate the mechanism, collapsing the buckler back into its bracer configuration.

**[HB] Hands Free.** Catarina's Fan does not occupy a hand in either form. The wearer may wield weapons two-handed, cast spells with somatic components, hold objects, or perform any action requiring free hands while the shield is deployed.

> [!DM]
> This item replaces Perrin's current standard shield, freeing his off-hand entirely. The practical effect: he can cast somatic spells, use the bodhran, or hold Clyde's Bestiary while still benefiting from shield AC. The bonus action cost for deployment means he can't both activate it and cast a leveled spell in the same turn — that tension is intentional.

## History

Catarina Da'Virelli built this as a commission, or a prototype, or both — the records in her workshop are intentionally unclear on the distinction. The mechanism is an extension of the same clockwork-and-Antheri-material methodology she developed after the Shelfworks goldrush. The Antheri fabric is a salvaged remnant from that original cache: near-indestructible, flexible when uncharged, and rigid as hardwood the instant current passes through it.

The origami folding is Da'Virelli's own innovation. The fabric compresses more tightly than any woven material has a right to, which is why the deployed buckler is large enough to function as a real shield while the collapsed bracer is small enough for a wrist. She will not explain how it folds, and the mechanism is sealed.

The size calibration is notable: it was built for a small frame. She does not comment on this.
